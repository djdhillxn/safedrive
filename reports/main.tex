\begin{abstract}
\noindent
SafeRL-Drive studies whether a continuous-control policy that has learned procedural road
geometry can adapt to moving traffic without forgetting traffic-free driving. The learned
Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) policy controls one ego vehicle; MetaDrive's native traffic
manager controls all surrounding vehicles. Earlier controls established a reproducible
pipeline and showed that a staged geometry curriculum raises held-out success from 12\%
to 87\% relative to equal-budget direct SAC. The same frozen curriculum policies achieved
69\% success but 21\% collision under a zero-shot density-0.05 traffic shift, motivating
one bounded extension. Two seed-0 warm-start pilots differ only in collision penalty; a
predeclared rule selects one for a seed-1 confirmation. Final evaluation pairs both
lineages on 100 fixed scenarios at densities 0, 0.05, and 0.10 and reports success,
collision, route completion, and traffic-free retention. This document distinguishes
the verified geometry evidence from traffic results that still require the planned Colab
runs; it makes no real-world safety claim.
\end{abstract}
\vspace{0.7em}
]

\section{Introduction}
Closed-loop simulation exposes a driving policy to the consequences of its own actions.
MetaDrive combines procedural road generation, reactive background traffic, physics, and
vector observations in a lightweight environment suited to generalization experiments
\cite{li2022metadrive}. SafeRL-Drive uses these features for a narrow student research
question:

\begin{quote}\small
\emph{Can a geometry-competent SAC policy adapt to interactive procedural traffic while
reducing collisions and retaining traffic-free road-generalization ability?}
\end{quote}

The claim is intentionally smaller than autonomous driving. The project does not model
real sensors, perception uncertainty, pedestrians, adverse weather, or deployment risk.
It tests reproducible learning and retention in one simulator.

\section{Single-Agent Traffic Formulation}
The task is single-agent reinforcement learning. SAC controls one ego vehicle with
continuous steering and throttle/brake. MetaDrive independently spawns and controls
background traffic with its native policies. Every condition fixes
\texttt{num\_agents=1} and \texttt{is\_multi\_agent=false}; no surrounding policy is
trained.

The policy receives MetaDrive's default bounded LidarState vector: ego state, navigation
and road features, and lidar information about nearby vehicles. Training never uses an
RGB observation or CNN. A separate recording environment activates the moving main
camera while retaining the same vector observation for policy inference.

All tasks use three-block procedural maps, a 1,000-step horizon, fixed spawn lanes,
continuous full-range actions, and zero accident probability. Training cycles through
200 scenarios from seed 40000. Validation starts at 50000; the held-out test starts at
60000. Evaluation traffic is deterministic conditional on its scenario seed
(\texttt{random\_traffic=false}, \texttt{traffic\_mode=respawn}).

\section{Geometry Curriculum as Initialization}
Phase 1 established the infrastructure on an intentionally easy straight, traffic-free
road. IDM, PPO, and SAC each reached 100\% success on 20 held-out episodes. Because PPO
used a discrete action grid while SAC used continuous control, these were pipeline
controls rather than a fair algorithm ranking.

Phase 2 asked whether staged road geometry helps SAC generalize. Direct SAC trained on
three-block procedural maps. Curriculum SAC trained first on curves, then
straight-plus-curve roads, and finally on the same procedural target. Architecture,
reward, action space, maximum 500,000-step budget, and test split matched.

The curriculum improved success by 75 percentage points and route completion by 48

retained as negative evidence. These results motivate warm start, not a claim that the
optimizer is uniformly reliable.

The frozen policies were then evaluated without traffic training at density 0.05.
Direct SAC averaged 3.5\% success and 37\% collision. Curriculum SAC averaged 69\%
success, 21\% collision, 6\% off-road, and 87.1\% route completion. This is promising
transfer but not traffic competence.

\section{Traffic-Adaptation Design}
The extension uses exactly three long runs. Both seed-0 pilots start from the corresponding
best geometry-curriculum checkpoint and receive fresh traffic replay buffers. Their only
intended learning difference is collision penalty:

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Controlled seed-0 traffic pilots.}
\label{tab:pilots}
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
Setting & Reference & Safety \\
\midrule
Vehicle collision penalty & 5 & 10 \\
Object collision penalty & 5 & 10 \\
Success reward & 10 & 10 \\
Off-road penalty & 5 & 5 \\
New transitions & 300k & 300k \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Each pilot uses 100,000 maximum transitions at density 0.02 followed by 200,000 at
density 0.05. Stage A must reach 70\% success, 80\% route completion, at most 25\%
collision, and at most 15\% off-road on 25 fixed validation scenarios. A miss pauses
the lineage; it does not trigger another pilot.

SAC~\cite{haarnoja2018sac} is implemented with
Stable-Baselines3~\cite{raffin2021sb3} and uses an MLP with two 256-unit layers,
learning rate $10^{-4}$, a 300,000-transition
buffer, 5,000 learning-start transitions, batch size 512, discount 0.99, target smoothing
0.005, fixed entropy coefficient 0.05, and one-step update frequency. Parallel simulator
processes are capped at four, and gradient steps equal the number of environments so the
update-to-data ratio remains approximately stable.

At the end of seed 0, both pilots are evaluated at densities 0, 0.05, and 0.10 on 50
validation scenarios. Qualification at density 0.05 requires at least 80\% success and
90\% route completion, at most 10\% collision and off-road, and no more than a ten-point
traffic-free success loss. If both qualify, lower collision wins, with success and then
route completion as declared tie-breakers. If neither qualifies, higher primary success
wins unless within five points, when lower collision wins. Only the selected condition is
confirmed from the seed-1 geometry checkpoint.

\section{Evaluation Protocol}
Frozen source and selected adapted policies from seeds 0 and 1 are evaluated on identical
scenario IDs at densities 0, 0.05, and 0.10. IDMPolicy and ExpertPolicy run on the same
matrix. Each policy--density condition contains 100 deterministic episodes.

Per-episode output retains MetaDrive's overlapping raw flags and adds one exclusive
terminal outcome: success, collision, off-road, timeout, or other. Summaries include a
Wilson 95\% interval for success; aggregate and vehicle-specific collision; collision-free,
off-road, and timeout rates; return, length, cost, speed, steering magnitude, action
change, and route completion. Environment, reward, action, split, and training metadata
are hashed. Comparisons fail closed when task definitions differ.

The primary contrasts are density-0.05 success gain and collision change. Retention is the
change in density-0 success and route completion. Density 0.10 measures stress degradation,
not a tuning target. Means and sample standard deviations are reported only across
completed adaptation lineages; a failed gate remains labeled diagnostic evidence rather
than being averaged as zero or hidden.

\section{Reproducibility Contract}
Each training run writes an immutable resolved configuration, source-lineage record,
source checkpoint SHA-256 digest, curriculum state, selected and resumable models, and
its own traffic replay buffer. The warm start transfers actor, critic, and target-network
parameters into a newly constructed SAC instance; it intentionally does not transfer the
source optimizer or traffic-free replay distribution. Observation and action shapes,
dtypes, and bounds must match before any parameter is loaded.

The runner may occupy up to four independent MetaDrive subprocesses, but never places
multiple engines in one process. A paused traffic run resumes only with its recorded
environment count, gradient-step count, reward variant, and stage definition. Completed
runs are reused and never overwritten. Atomic metadata and latest-pointer replacement
let a restarted notebook distinguish a resumable run from a mismatched or completed one.

Evaluation artifacts include full episode CSVs and condition summaries. A fingerprint
binds map, traffic, horizon, reward, action interface, test split, controller, and
training context. Existing results are reused only on an exact fingerprint match.
Training also records package and hardware versions plus periodic CPU, RAM, GPU-memory,
and throughput samples. Critical artifacts are copied from the live Colab clone to Drive
after every long run and verified at the destination.

\section{Execution Gates and Public Artifacts}
No adaptation begins until two non-learning gates pass. First, native IDMPolicy and
ExpertPolicy each run 50 fixed validation scenarios at densities 0.05 and 0.10. This
checks task solvability and establishes controller ceilings without treating either
hand-engineered policy as a learned baseline. Second, ExpertPolicy produces an actual
headless chase-camera frame sequence. A rendering failure stops execution rather than
silently substituting an easier top-down view.

The complete workflow lives in one Colab notebook with 20 ordered sections: runtime and
Drive setup; compilation and smoke gates; historical summaries; native baselines; frozen
source matrices; the two seed-0 pilots; saved variant selection; seed-1 confirmation;
final matrices; videos; plots; reports; and final synchronization. Long cells consult
latest pointers and curriculum state, so a disconnected runtime can restore artifacts
and continue at the next unfinished section.

Five final video roles are declared before seeing their appearance. The frozen and
adapted policies share the lowest evaluated density-0.05 scenario seed for a direct
before/after comparison. The success video uses the lowest successful adapted scenario;
the diagnostic failure uses the lowest failed one when any failure exists. ExpertPolicy
provides the native demonstration. Every MP4 has a JSON sidecar with model, selection
rule, scenario, density, outcome, return, completion, collision, frame count, camera
settings, and experiment fingerprint.

The public artifact set is deliberately small: five comparison figures, seed-level and
aggregate CSV/JSON tables, the selection JSON, selected videos, and this report. This
keeps the conclusion tied to the research question instead of turning implementation
diagnostics into additional experiments.

\section{Predeclared Interpretation}
The study separates three claims that are easy to blur. The exposure claim asks whether
the adapted policy improves density-0.05 success relative to its own frozen source. The
safety-ablation claim asks whether doubling only vehicle and object collision penalties
changes the success--collision trade-off under identical seed-0 data and optimization.
The retention claim asks whether either improvement preserves the source policy's
traffic-free success and route completion. Density 0.10 is reported as stress
generalization and never participates in checkpoint selection.

A low collision rate alone is not positive evidence. A policy that brakes indefinitely
will appear collision-free but will lose success, completion, and episode length through
timeouts. The qualifying gate therefore requires all four primary behaviors. Conversely,
a success increase accompanied by greater collision may be useful learning evidence but
does not answer the stated safety question. The report retains both values instead of
collapsing them into one reward score.

The reward comparison is exploratory at seed 0 because it selects the confirmation
variant. Only the winning rule is confirmed at seed 1, so the final estimate describes
two adapted lineages rather than an unbiased two-seed comparison of both rewards. Native
controllers establish solvability ceilings, not statistical competitors: they use
simulator-specific control logic and do not share SAC's learned action mapping.

The result remains interpretable if all gates fail. In that case the evidence says that
this bounded warm-start and reward intervention did not reliably solve interactive
traffic under the allotted 300,000-transition budget. The protocol does not add another
seed, tune a third penalty, or replace the architecture after observing failure. This
stopping rule is central to keeping a negative result as credible as a positive one.

\section{Failure Analysis and Retention}
Three outcomes are scientifically useful. First, adaptation may reduce collision while
preserving source performance, supporting the proposed transfer. Second, a policy may
improve traffic success but lose traffic-free geometry competence, revealing catastrophic
forgetting. Third, both reward variants may miss the gate; the predeclared selection still
produces one confirmation and reports the miss honestly rather than expanding the search.

The video set uses declared scenario rules. A matched before/after pair uses the same
lowest held-out seed. Representative success and diagnostic failure videos use the lowest
seed satisfying that outcome in the evaluation CSV. The public default is a moving
1280-by-720 chase view; top-down rendering remains diagnostic only.

\section{Limitations}
Two training seeds cannot characterize optimizer variance. Traffic density is simulator
specific, and built-in traffic policies are not a proxy for human behavior. Procedural
maps remain short, observations are privileged low-dimensional simulator state, and
validation participates in checkpoint and variant selection. Wilson intervals describe
episode sampling uncertainty, not uncertainty across training runs. Most importantly,
collision reduction in MetaDrive is not evidence of real-world autonomous-driving safety.

\balance
\section{Conclusion}
SafeRL-Drive first established trustworthy artifacts, then showed that geometry curriculum
can materially improve procedural-road generalization. The zero-shot traffic failure
provides a concrete final question rather than an excuse for an open-ended experiment
sweep. The fixed three-run extension tests warm-start traffic exposure, one controlled
reward ablation, retention, and stress degradation. Its value lies in the bounded design
and auditable result, whether the final answer is positive or negative.
