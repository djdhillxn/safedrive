\begin{abstract}
SafeRL-Drive is a reproducible student study of closed-loop reinforcement learning built
with MetaDrive and Stable-Baselines3. Phase 1 trains PPO and SAC on a
single traffic-free straight-road task, selects checkpoints on fixed validation seeds,
evaluates frozen policies on disjoint test seeds, and compares them with MetaDrive's
rule-based IDM policy. The benchmark records
success, collision, off-road, timeout, route-completion, return, cost, speed, and episode
length metrics while retaining the configurations, logs, models, and plots needed to
reproduce each result. Numerical results in this scaffold are intentionally left as
placeholders until the final Colab experiments are complete.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Closed-loop simulation exposes a driving policy to the consequences of its own actions.
This makes it useful for studying control failures that are hidden by one-step prediction
metrics. SafeRL-Drive focuses on a deliberately small question: can two standard deep
reinforcement-learning algorithms reliably learn a basic driving task under
the same observations, rewards, and evaluation metrics? MetaDrive supplies the
configurable simulator used for this study \cite{li2022metadrive}.

\section{Scope and Success Criteria}
The project is an engineering proof of learning, not a claim of production autonomous
driving. Phase 1 is complete only if the frozen best checkpoint for each learned agent
achieves at least 80\% success and 90\% mean route completion, with no more than 10\%
collision, off-road, and timeout outcomes on 20 untouched test episodes. The required deliverables
are reproducible PPO, SAC, and IDM artifacts; auditable evaluation tables; learning and
outcome plots; one rollout video per learned agent; and a concise report. Curved roads,
procedural-map generalization, traffic, image observations, and explicit safe-RL methods
are ordered extensions, not hidden prerequisites for the Phase-1 claim. PPO uses a
$3\times3$ discrete steering/throttle grid, while SAC is continuous with steering limited
to $[-0.1,0.1]$. These are two learning controls, not a fair algorithm-ranking experiment;
continuous PPO and unrestricted SAC are explicit robustness extensions.

\section{Problem Setup}
The agent receives MetaDrive's bounded vector observation and produces steering and
throttle/brake commands. Phase 1 uses one straight topology, fixed spawning, no traffic,
a 500-step horizon, and MetaDrive's reference reward scale. Training begins at seed 0.
Validation uses ten seeds beginning at 1000 and is used only for checkpoint selection.
Final evaluation uses 20 seeds beginning at 4000 and is not consulted until the checkpoint
is frozen. Straight-block parameters may vary with the seed, but road topology and the
no-traffic setting remain fixed. The split therefore must not be described as broad
procedural generalization. Seeds 3000--3019 were consumed during local diagnosis.

\section{Environment and Agents}
The learning agents are Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) \cite{schulman2017ppo} and
Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) \cite{haarnoja2018sac}, implemented through
Stable-Baselines3 \cite{raffin2021sb3}. Both use the same MLP architecture, but their
documented action interfaces differ. PPO uses MetaDrive's beginner $3\times3$ discrete
steering/throttle grid, four collection processes, and CPU learning.
SAC uses one environment, GPU acceleration when available, and no saved replay buffer.
Its entropy coefficient is fixed after automatic tuning collapsed during a diagnostic;
fixed entropy alone did not solve unrestricted steering exploration, so its straight-road
control limits steering to $[-0.1,0.1]$.
MetaDrive's \texttt{IDMPolicy} is evaluated without training as the rule-based reference.

\section{Experimental Design}
Each learning algorithm receives at most 100,000 environment timesteps. Validation runs
every 25,000 steps. When a checkpoint reaches 80\% success and 90\% route completion while
keeping collision, off-road, and timeout rates at or below 10\%, it is saved before training stops.
Otherwise, the run ends at the budget and is diagnosed rather
than silently extended. The best checkpoint is selected lexicographically by success,
route completion, lower off-road/collision/timeout rates, and return. Only the frozen
winner receives held-out testing. The smoke test is excluded from reported results.
Callback evaluation seeds only its simulator subprocess; it does not reset Python, NumPy,
or PyTorch in the parent training process.

MetaDrive's reference dense progress, speed, and terminal rewards are used without custom
SafeDrive shaping. This is intentional: the earlier long-horizon reward made braking and
stalling locally attractive, while continuous PPO's default exploration saturated steering.
The bounded observation is used directly. SAC's explicit steering bound prevents its replay
buffer from filling with full-lock inputs on a straight road; PPO uses the documented
discrete beginner abstraction. Horizon exhaustion
is treated as an artificial Gymnasium truncation for value learning, while timeout remains
a reported task failure.

\section{Metrics}
The headline metrics are success rate, collision rate, off-road rate, timeout rate, and
mean route completion. Mean return and episode length summarize task performance and
rollout duration. Mean safety cost and speed provide additional context. Success rate is
accompanied by a 95\% Wilson confidence interval. Per-episode CSV files retain scenario
seeds, base and shaped returns, full-rollout action statistics, and final simulator
information so aggregate values remain auditable.

\section{Discussion}
The final discussion must distinguish task return from driving safety. A controller can
make progress while still leaving the road, and a conservative policy can avoid incidents
by timing out. The rule-based baseline helps determine whether learned behavior is merely
active or completes the task. Strong results on the fixed straight road demonstrate
pipeline competence and basic longitudinal/lateral control only.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
Phase 1 uses one training run per algorithm, a fixed road geometry, state-vector
observations, one simulator, and a small evaluation budget. Results therefore cannot
establish procedural generalization or statistical superiority. The next experiment changes
only the action interface: continuous PPO and unrestricted SAC steering on the same straight
road. The following experiment changes only the map to a fixed curve. A small traffic-free
procedural set follows only after both agents
pass that gate; traffic and explicit safety mechanisms remain later work.

\section{Conclusion}
SafeRL-Drive connects training, held-out evaluation, rule-based comparison, visualization,
video, and artifact logging in one reproducible workflow. Its bounded Phase-1 claim and
ordered difficulty ladder prevent weak broad experiments from being mistaken for working
autonomous driving. The final report will replace the placeholders with measured PPO, SAC,
and IDM results from the saved comparison files.
